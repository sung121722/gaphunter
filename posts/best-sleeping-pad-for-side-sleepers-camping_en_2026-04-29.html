What Actually Matters When Choosing the Best Sleeping Pad for Side Sleepers Camping

1. Thickness — And Not Just Any Number

Side sleepers need a minimum of 3 inches (about 7.5 cm) of loft. Below that, your hip bone will bottom out on hard or semi-hard ground by hour three. The 9 cm pad above hits the sweet spot. Memory foam can get away with slightly less because it compresses differently than air or open-cell foam — but anything under 2.5 inches of memory foam is still a gamble.

2. Width — Wider Than You Think You Need

Standard pads run 20–22 inches wide. That's fine if you sleep perfectly still in one position all night. Nobody does. Side sleepers especially shift — hip drop, arm extension, partial rollover. 25 inches is a reasonable minimum. 72 cm (28 inches) like the 2026 upgraded pad is genuinely better. If you've ever woken up half on the pad and half on the tent floor, width is why.

3. Deflation and Firmness Control

For air pads, your ability to control firmness is everything for side sleeping. A pad inflated rock-hard is basically the ground with extra steps. You want enough air to keep your body off the floor but soft enough that your shoulder and hip have somewhere to go. Pads with easy valve control — not tiny finicky valves — make this calibration actually possible in a dark tent at 10 p.m.

4. Ground Insulation (R-value) vs. Comfort Padding

These are different things. R-value tells you how well the pad insulates from ground cold — critical for spring and fall camping, less so for July trips. Comfort padding tells you how your pressure points feel. Don't assume a high R-value pad is automatically comfortable for side sleeping, or that a soft foam pad will keep you warm in 40°F nights. For peak summer camping (May–August), comfort is often the priority; for shoulder seasons, you need both.

5. Actual Packed Size vs. What the Listing Claims

The Outdoorsman Lab pad packs to 8×3 inches — that's a real, verified number. Other pads list "compact" without giving dimensions. Before you buy, find the actual packed dimensions. A pad that "packs compactly" but is still 24 inches long rolled up isn't solving your pack organization problem. Ask specifically: what are the packed dimensions?

Frequently Asked Questions

What thickness sleeping pad do I need as a side sleeper?

At minimum, 3 inches (7.5 cm) for most people. Side sleeping creates concentrated pressure at the hip and shoulder — those points need real cushion, not just insulation. Memory foam can work at slightly lower thickness due to its conforming properties, but air and standard foam pads should hit 3 inches or more. The 9 cm (about 3.5 inch) option in this guide is specifically designed with that in mind.

Can I use an ultralight sleeping pad if I'm a side sleeper?

Yes, but you have to manage inflation carefully. An ultralight air pad at medium inflation can absolutely work for side sleeping — the Outdoorsman Lab pad at 14.5 oz is proof. The key is not over-inflating. A rock-hard ultralight pad eliminates all pressure relief. Dial back the firmness until your shoulder and hip sink in slightly, and you'll sleep fine. Bring a patch kit.

Self-inflating vs. air pad — which is better for side sleepers?

Self-inflating pads have a foam core that provides more predictable, structured support — which many side sleepers prefer because there's no "bouncing" feeling. Air pads offer more thickness-to-weight ratio, meaning you can get more loft for less weight. If you're car camping, lean toward self-inflating for consistency. If you're backpacking, an air pad gives you more cushion without destroying your pack weight.

How wide should a sleeping pad be for side sleeping?

25 inches minimum, 28+ inches ideal. Side sleepers extend arms, drop hips sideways, and partially roll — all of which eat up horizontal pad space. Standard 20-22 inch pads work if you're a disciplined back-sleeper, but if you know you move around, the wider 72 cm (28 inch) options make a meaningful difference in how often you wake up half off the pad.

Is a foam or inflatable sleeping pad better for camping in summer 2026?

For summer camping specifically, insulation (R-value) matters less than comfort because ground temperatures are warmer. That means you can prioritize cushion over thermal protection. Foam pads — especially memory foam — deliver excellent pressure relief and never deflate mid-night. Air pads give you more loft in a smaller packed size. Either works for summer; the choice really comes down to whether you're driving to camp (foam is fine) or hiking in (air pad wins on weight).
