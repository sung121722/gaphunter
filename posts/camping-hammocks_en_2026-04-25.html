Buying Guide: What Actually Matters

1. Weight Capacity — and Why the Margin Matters

Never buy a hammock rated at or just above your body weight. A hammock rated 250 lbs for a 230 lb person is a liability. Fabric, stitching, and hardware all degrade over time and under repeated tension. Look for a minimum of 300 lbs of headroom above your body weight. The Wise Owl (500 lbs) and MOSFiATA (550 lbs) both have wide enough margins to sleep safely for years.

2. Fabric Type — Nylon vs. Canvas

Standard ripstop nylon (often called "parachute fabric") is lighter, packs down smaller, and dries fast. It's the right call for backpackers. Canvas or thickened polyester like the MOSFiATA's 320G fabric is softer against bare skin, holds its shape better over time, and is more comfortable for multi-hour lounging — but it's heavier and slower to dry. Match fabric to your use case: nylon for trail, canvas for camp.

3. Hammock Dimensions — Length AND Width

Most people focus on length and ignore width. That's a mistake. A hammock needs to be wide enough to sleep diagonally — the only position that keeps your back flat instead of curved. Anything under 55 inches wide starts restricting your diagonal angle, especially for people over 6 feet tall or with broader shoulders. The MOSFiATA's 61-inch width is one of the widest in this category.

For length: a 9–10 foot (108–120 inch) hammock is the sweet spot for most adults. The Wise Owl's 120-inch length (10 feet) is generous enough to hang at the proper 30-degree angle without the fabric bottom pulling your knees up.

4. Included Straps — Width and Length

Straps matter for two reasons: safety and campsite access. Many national parks, state parks, and forest service areas require straps to be at least 0.75 inches (¾ inch) wide to prevent bark damage. Thin straps can get you turned away at the trailhead or fined at a campsite. Strap length determines how far apart your trees can be — look for straps that are at least 9–10 feet each. Both Wise Owl and MOSFiATA include straps, which removes that guesswork.

5. Hardware — Carabiners and Loops

Check the carabiner rating. A hammock carabiner rated under 200 lbs is a red flag on anything marketed for camping. Quality aluminum carabiners on camp hammocks are typically rated 500–1,100 lbs. Steel is heavier but rated even higher. Also check whether the carabiner gate locks — an unlocked gate that accidentally opens mid-night is the kind of surprise nobody wants. Look for screwgate or auto-locking carabiners on anything you're sleeping in.

Frequently Asked Questions

What distance do trees need to be for a camping hammock?

The standard is 10 to 15 feet apart for most camping hammocks. Too close (under 8 feet) and you can't hang at the right angle — you'll end up nearly vertical. Too far (over 18–20 feet) and your straps won't reach, or the sag angle becomes extreme. Before picking a campsite, eyeball the distance: if you can take about 4–5 normal steps between the trees, you're in the right zone.

Can you use a camping hammock without trees?

Yes, with a hammock stand. Metal freestanding hammock stands (available in the Hammocks, Stands & Accessories category) let you set up anywhere — beach, desert, concrete campsite. Most stands handle hammocks up to 10 feet and support 400–500 lbs. They add weight and bulk, so they're a car camping solution, not a backpacking one.

Is sleeping in a camping hammock bad for your back?

Only if you hang it wrong. The common mistake is hanging too tight, which curves the hammock into a banana shape and forces your spine to follow. The fix is simple: hang with a 30-degree sag angle (the center of the hammock should drop roughly 18 inches below the attachment points) and sleep diagonally across the fabric, not straight down the center. Done right, many people actually report better sleep in a hammock than on a ground pad.

What temperature is a camping hammock good to?

A hammock alone (no insulation) works comfortably down to about 60–65°F at night. Below that, the cold air circulating underneath you pulls heat away much faster than sleeping on the ground. For temperatures in the 40s–50s°F, you need an underquilt (insulation that wraps beneath the hammock) or a sleeping pad stuffed inside. For anything below freezing, a purpose-made winter underquilt rated to the temperature plus a top quilt is the standard setup among cold-weather hammock campers.

How much should I spend on a first camping hammock?

You don't need to spend much to get a reliable hammock. A solid starter setup — hammock plus straps — typically runs $30–60. The Wise Owl Outfitters and MOSFiATA options both fall in an accessible range with straps included. Where people waste money early on is buying cheap hammocks without straps, then purchasing straps separately — ending up spending $50+ total on a lower-quality system. Buy complete from the start.
