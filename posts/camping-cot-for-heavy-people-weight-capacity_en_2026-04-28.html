What Actually Matters When Choosing a Camping Cot for Heavy People

1. Weight Capacity — And Then Add 100 Lbs

Manufacturers test cots at static load. You don't sleep like a bag of sand — you move, shift, sit up, occasionally have a dog jump on you. The real-world safe working load is roughly 85–90% of rated capacity. So if you weigh 300 lbs, you need a cot rated to at least 350–380 lbs minimum. Shop up, not at exactly your weight.

2. Frame Design Matters More Than Fabric Weight

The X-frame design on cots like the HABUTWAY distributes load across more contact points than a simple straight-bar frame. More legs, more triangulation, more resistance to lateral flex. When you're evaluating a cot for heavier use, look at the frame architecture first — fabric second.

3. Width Is Not Optional for Larger Campers

A 25-inch sleeping surface is a genuine problem for a broader body. You'll spend the night managing your sleeping position instead of actually sleeping. The REDCAMP at 59 inches is the extreme solution. But anything under 30 inches is going to feel punishing on a four-night trip.

4. Leg Stability and Ground Contact

Non-slip feet. More legs. Wider base. These all reduce the chance of a cot tipping or sliding on tent floors, packed dirt, or wooden tent platforms. For heavier users, the leverage involved when you sit on the edge of a cot is significant — a narrow base will tip. Check how many legs the cot has and how wide they spread.

5. Setup Time at Actual Body Weight

Some cots have spring-loaded setups that require significant hand force to lock. If you have larger hands or limited dexterity, test the mechanism description carefully. "Easy setup" on a product page sometimes means "easy if you weigh 160 lbs and have full grip strength." Real reviews from heavier users will tell you more than marketing copy.
