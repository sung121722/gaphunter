What Actually Matters When Choosing a Sleeping Pad for Side Sleepers

  • Thickness — the non-negotiable: Side sleeping concentrates your entire body weight onto one hip and one shoulder simultaneously. You need at least 3 inches (7.5cm) of loft to prevent bottoming out on hard ground. Anything under 2.5 inches will have you feeling the tent floor before midnight. The OutdoorGearLab team's testing (updated May 2026) consistently favors pads like the Therm-a-Rest NeoLoft specifically because it's 1 inch thicker than average — for side sleepers, that inch is the difference between a good night and a terrible one.
  • Width — wider than you think you need: Standard pads run about 20 inches wide. That's fine for back sleepers. Side sleepers shift positions, roll partially, and need margin. Look for 25–28 inches (65–72cm). If you drop off the edge of your pad at 3 a.m., you're waking up on the ground.
  • R-value and insulation: For summer camping, an R-value of 2–3 is workable. If you're camping into September or at elevation, go R-4 or higher. An uninsulated pad in 45°F ground temps will pull heat from your body all night, and no sleeping bag fully compensates for that. The electric-pump pad above is insulated — that matters even in summer at altitude.
  • Self-inflating vs. air pad vs. foam: Self-inflating pads use open-cell foam that pulls in air on its own — they're heavier but more puncture-resistant. Pure air pads pack smaller and get thicker, but one rogue stick through the floor voids your night. Foam is indestructible but bulky and thinner. For car camping side sleepers: self-inflating or thick air pad. For backpacking side sleepers: go air pad, accept the puncture risk, pack a patch kit.
  • Capacity and durability: If you're over 200 lbs, pad thickness matters even more because body weight compresses the pad further. Check the weight rating — pads rated for 300+ lbs maintain their loft better under a heavier sleeper. The electric-pump pad's 665 lb rating isn't marketing fluff; it signals the internal structure won't collapse in a way that cheaper pads do.

Frequently Asked Questions

How thick should a sleeping pad be for side sleepers?

At minimum, 3 inches (7.5cm). Most sleep researchers and gear testers agree that hip pressure from side sleeping requires more cushion than back sleeping does. If your pad is under 2.5 inches and you're waking up with hip or shoulder pain, thickness is your first fix — not a new pillow, not a different sleeping bag. The 3.14-inch and 9cm options in this list are both at or above the functional minimum for true side sleepers on firm ground.

Can I use an ultralight backpacking pad if I sleep on my side?

You can, but it's a compromise. Most ultralight pads (like the Klymit Static V or Nemo Tensor) run 2–2.5 inches thick and weigh under 1 lb. They're designed to minimize pack weight, not maximize hip cushion. Side sleepers on these pads often supplement with a thin closed-cell foam sit pad under the hip zone, or simply accept some discomfort in exchange for pack weight savings. If comfort is the priority over pack weight, stay above 3 inches of thickness.

Is a self-inflating pad better than an air pad for side sleepers?

For car camping: self-inflating wins. The open-cell foam inside resists full compression better than a pure air chamber, which means it's less likely to "bottom out" when your hip presses down hard. For backpacking: a thick air pad (3+ inches) wins on packability and weight. The tradeoff is puncture vulnerability. Always carry a patch kit. OutdoorGearLab's 2026 update notes that the Therm-a-Rest NeoLoft — a top-tier air pad at about 4 inches thick — is the most comfortable option available for side sleepers who refuse to compromise, though it runs significantly heavier than budget alternatives.

What width sleeping pad do side sleepers need?

Look for at least 25 inches (63cm), and 28 inches (72cm) if you're a restless sleeper or broad-shouldered. Standard pads at 20 inches are genuinely too narrow — your shoulder hangs off the edge in your widest sleeping position. The 72cm-wide 2026 edition in this list is a direct response to this complaint; that extra width was the top user request in the previous model's reviews.

Do sleeping pads help with hip pain when camping?

Yes — meaningfully. Hip pain during camping almost always traces back to ground pressure on the greater trochanter (the bony outer point of your hip). A pad thick enough to prevent your hip from "bottoming out" to the ground eliminates most of that pressure. A pad rated R-3 or higher also prevents the conductive cold from hard, cold ground from stiffening your hip flexors overnight. If you're already dealing with hip issues, prioritize 3+ inches of thickness and a pad wide enough that you're not sleeping half on, half off.
