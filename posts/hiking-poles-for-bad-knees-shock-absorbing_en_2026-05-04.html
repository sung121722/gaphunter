What Actually Matters When Choosing Hiking Poles for Bad Knees

  • Spring mechanism quality, not just spring presence: Cheap poles list "anti-shock" as a feature but use a single rubber bumper with zero travel distance. A real shock-absorbing system has 5–15mm of spring travel, absorbs the initial impact spike, and doesn't bottom out on rocky terrain. Ask yourself: does the product description specify the mechanism, or just say "anti-shock"? Vague = skip it.
  • Pole length and your height: For bad knees on descents, most physios recommend shortening your poles 5–10cm below the standard elbow-bend position. This shifts more load onto the poles and less onto your quads and knee joint. Adjustable poles (not fixed-length) are non-negotiable if your terrain varies.
  • Shaft material and lateral stiffness: 7075 aluminum is stronger than the more common 6061 alloy. Carbon fiber is lighter but can snap under sudden lateral force — risky if you're using poles as a recovery aid, not just a rhythm tool. For bad knees where the poles are load-bearing, 7075 aluminum is the safer call.
  • Grip material and geometry: Cork handles wick sweat but are rigid and unforgiving on long days. EVA foam grips absorb micro-vibrations and stay grippy when wet. For knee-pain hikers who need consistent pole-plant force, EVA foam is more forgiving and maintains technique across a full day.
  • Tip and basket combo: A worn rubber tip reduces traction and makes pole plants unpredictable. On technical terrain, that unpredictability causes overcompensation in your stride — which loads the knee at bad angles. Check that replacement tips are available for whatever poles you buy. Carbide tips grip better on rock and last longer than standard steel.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do shock-absorbing trekking poles actually reduce knee pain, or is that marketing?

The research is real. A 1999 study in the Journal of Sports Medicine (and several replications since) found that trekking poles reduce compressive knee forces by 12–25% on downhill gradients depending on pole technique. The shock-absorbing spring compounds this by damping the initial impact spike — the moment of heel-strike on a downhill step — before that force travels up the chain to your knee. It's not a cure for existing knee damage, but it's a legitimate, measurable load-reduction tool. The caveat: you have to actually use the poles with weight on them. Passive pole touching does almost nothing. Drive through the grip with each step.

What's the correct pole height setting for someone with bad knees specifically?

Standard advice is to set poles so your elbow bends at 90 degrees on flat ground. For bad knees on descents, drop 5–10cm below that. This creates a longer reach forward, encourages you to plant the pole ahead of your body, and loads the pole — not your knee — as the braking force on each downhill step. On climbs, raise the poles back to the standard 90-degree position so you're driving upward efficiently. Adjustable telescoping poles that you can change in 5 seconds mid-trail are worth the minor weight penalty over fixed-length options.

Is 7075 aluminum actually worth it over regular aluminum for trekking poles?

Yes, and the difference matters specifically for people using poles as a load-bearing knee-pain tool. 7075 aluminum has a tensile strength of roughly 570 MPa versus 310 MPa for the more common 6061 alloy. In practical terms, a 7075 pole at the same wall thickness is significantly more resistant to bending under lateral load — the exact stress that occurs when you plant a pole on a sloped surface and push your weight through it. You're not just hiking with these. You're braking with them. You want the extra stiffness. The VVHU poles at 250g (8.8 oz) per pole are a rare case where you get 7075 alloy at a budget-friendly price point.

Can I use hiking poles if I have a meniscus tear or have had knee surgery?

Poles are frequently recommended by orthopedic physios post-surgery as part of return-to-activity protocols, specifically because they offload compressive forces from the knee joint during uneven terrain. That said, "hiking poles help" isn't blanket clearance to push the same mileage you did pre-injury. The poles reduce load per step — they don't eliminate it. If you're within 12 months of a meniscus repair, get your surgeon's sign-off on trail gradient and mileage before going beyond flat walking. Nordic walking poles (slightly longer, with specialized straps) are also worth considering post-surgery as the forward arm-drive technique further reduces knee torque.

Are collapsible folding poles less durable than telescoping twist-lock poles for knee support?

Modern folding poles using a tensioned cord (like most ultralight collapsible designs) are structurally sound for most trail conditions and match telescoping poles on compressive load. The weak point isn't the folding mechanism — it's the cord tension over time. A cord that's been compressed 300+ times may develop micro-slack, which creates very slight wobble at the fold joints. That wobble isn't dangerous, but it's annoying and slightly reduces shock-transmission efficiency. Re-tensioning the cord is a 2-minute fix on most designs. For knee-pain hiking specifically, twist-lock or lever-lock telescoping poles offer marginally better rigidity under sustained heavy braking loads — but the difference is small enough that the packability of folding poles wins for most people.
