## Buying Guide: What Actually Matters
**Weight** matters only if you're carrying more than half a mile. Under that distance,
comfort and durability outrank grams. The ultralight options (under 2 lbs) sacrifice
seat depth, which becomes uncomfortable after 45 minutes.

**Seat height** is the most overlooked spec. Anything under 12 inches puts your knees
above your hips — fine for 20 minutes, painful for an evening. Measure your knee height
while seated and match accordingly.

**Weight capacity**: manufacturers typically test at 150% of stated capacity.
A "250 lb" chair is actually safe at 375 lbs static weight — but dynamic loading
(sitting down hard) creates brief spikes 2-3x your body weight.

**Pack size** matters for car campers only if trunk space is limited.
For backpackers, anything over 4 inches diameter won't fit in a standard pack side pocket.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**What is the most comfortable camping chair?**
For extended sitting (3+ hours), chairs with a seat height of 16-18 inches and
a reclined back angle of 100-110 degrees rank highest in user comfort studies.
The Kijaro Dual Lock fits this profile at $60.

**Are expensive camping chairs worth it?**
Above $100, you're paying for weight reduction and longevity — not comfort.
A $40 chair and a $200 chair feel nearly identical for the first season.
After three seasons, the $200 chair typically still functions; the $40 one often doesn't.

**How much weight can camping chairs hold?**
Most standard chairs handle 225-275 lbs. Heavy-duty options (Kamp-Rite, KingCamp)
support 350-800 lbs. Always check the stated capacity — it's usually printed on the frame.

**Can camping chairs get wet?**
Most use polyester or nylon that dries quickly. Avoid leaving them wet in storage;
the metal frames, particularly at joints, will corrode within one season if stored damp.
