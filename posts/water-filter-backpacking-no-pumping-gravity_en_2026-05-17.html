What Actually Matters When Choosing

  • Filtration rating (microns): You want 0.1 microns or finer. That's the threshold for removing bacteria and protozoa. Every filter on this list hits 0.1 microns — don't settle for anything looser.
  • Bag volume vs. group size: 3 liters works for one person for a night. Two people or a full day of cooking? Go 6L minimum. Four or more people at a basecamp? The MSR AutoFlow XL's 10L bag is the honest answer.
  • Flow rate under real conditions: Manufacturer flow rates are tested with clean water. In the field, muddy alpine lakes or silt-heavy streams will cut that rate significantly. Pre-filtering through a bandana or letting sediment settle first makes a real difference.
  • Weight vs. convenience tradeoff: The lightest option (SimPure at 2.8 oz) is great for solo trips. Larger bags with reinforced straps add weight but save you constant refills. Know what you're optimizing for before you buy.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a gravity water filter take to filter water?

It depends on the filter and water clarity. The MSR AutoFlow XL is the fastest on this list at 1.75 liters per minute — a full 10L bag filtered in roughly 6 minutes. The Membrane Solutions Pro filters at about 0.5L per minute, so a full 6L bag takes around 12 minutes. Turbid, silty water will slow any gravity filter down — pre-filtering through a cloth or letting the bag settle first gets you closer to rated speed.

Can gravity water filters remove viruses?

No — standard hollow fiber gravity filters at 0.1 microns remove bacteria (99.9999%) and protozoa (99.9%), but viruses are smaller than 0.1 microns and pass through. In North America and most backcountry destinations, viruses in natural water sources are a low risk. If you're traveling internationally or filtering water in areas with high human activity, pair a gravity filter with chemical treatment (iodine or chlorine dioxide tablets) or upgrade to a filter with a built-in chemical stage. None of the gravity filters on this list alone address viruses.

How do you clean a gravity water filter in the field?

Most hollow fiber gravity filters clean by backflushing — push clean water backward through the membrane to dislodge trapped sediment. The SimPure and Membrane Solutions both support this. The MSR AutoFlow XL cleans by agitating clean water inside the filter housing and flushing it out. Do it whenever flow rate drops noticeably — usually every few days on heavy use. Never let a hollow fiber filter freeze while wet; ice crystals rupture the fibers and permanently destroy the filter.

What's the difference between a gravity filter and a squeeze filter for backpacking?

A gravity filter hangs passively — you fill a bag, hang it above your water vessel, and let gravity do the work while you do something else. A squeeze filter (like the Katadyn BeFree) requires you to actively squeeze the reservoir to push water through. Gravity filters win for camp use and cooking water. Squeeze filters win for on-the-go hydration when you don't want to stop and hang a bag. Some filters (like the SimPure) function as both, which gives you flexibility without carrying two systems.

Are gravity water filters reliable enough for a multi-week backpacking trip?

Yes, with proper maintenance. The SimPure is rated to 5,000 liters — more than enough for a 30-day trip even with heavy use. The MSR AutoFlow XL has a 3-year limited warranty and is built for sustained basecamp use. The main field failure mode is frozen filters (never store them wet in freezing temps) or membrane tears from rough handling. Carry a backup — either chemical tablets or a second squeeze filter — and check the membrane integrity if you ever suspect a drop or hard impact damaged the housing.
