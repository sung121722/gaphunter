Buying Guide: What Actually Matters

Temperature Rating Reality Check

Temperature ratings aren't standardized, so treat them as rough guidelines. EN (European Norm) ratings provide more consistent testing, but many bags still use manufacturer ratings. Add 10-15 degrees to any stated rating for realistic comfort. A 20°F bag will keep most people comfortable around 35°F, not 20°F.

Consider your personal sleep temperature too. Cold sleepers need bags rated 10-20 degrees below expected nighttime lows. Warm sleepers can get away with bags closer to actual temperatures.

Down vs Synthetic Fill

Down insulation packs smaller and weighs less but loses insulation when wet and costs more. Synthetic fill handles moisture better, dries faster, and costs less but weighs more and packs larger. For car camping, synthetic makes sense. For backpacking where weight matters, down wins despite the higher price.

Down fill power ratings (like 650-fill or 800-fill) indicate loft quality. Higher numbers mean better insulation per weight, but also higher prices.

Shape and Size Considerations

Mummy bags minimize weight and maximize warmth but feel restrictive. Rectangular bags offer more room but weigh more and don't retain heat as well. Semi-rectangular bags split the difference.

Length matters — too short and your feet will be cold, too long and you'll have dead air space reducing efficiency. Most manufacturers offer regular and long sizes. Width affects comfort but also thermal efficiency.

Weight and Packability

For backpacking, aim for under 3 pounds, ideally under 2.5 pounds. Car campers can prioritize comfort over weight. Compression sacks help reduce packed size but check the compressed dimensions before assuming it'll fit your pack.

Stuff sacks versus compression sacks make a difference. Basic stuff sacks just contain the bag. Compression sacks use straps or zippers to squeeze out air, reducing volume by 20-30%.

Quality Indicators

Check zipper quality — YKK zippers generally outlast cheaper alternatives. Draft tubes along zippers prevent heat loss. Hood and footbox design affects warmth retention. Reinforced stress points extend bag life.

Fabric denier indicates thickness — 20D fabric balances weight and durability for most users. Thinner fabrics save weight but tear more easily.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I choose the right temperature rating?

Take the coldest temperature you expect to encounter and subtract 10-15 degrees. That's your target temperature rating. For example, if nighttime lows will be 40°F, look for bags rated to 25-30°F. This accounts for rating inconsistencies and personal variation in cold tolerance.

Can I wash my sleeping bag?

Yes, but follow manufacturer instructions carefully. Use front-loading machines or large top-loaders without agitators. Use specialized down wash for down bags or gentle detergent for synthetic. Add tennis balls or dryer balls during drying to restore loft. Air dry partially before using heat settings.

How long do sleeping bags last?

Quality sleeping bags last 5-10 years with regular use. Down bags last longer than synthetic if properly maintained. Signs of replacement include compressed insulation that won't loft, fabric tears, or broken zippers. Store bags loose, not compressed, to extend life.

What's the difference between men's and women's sleeping bags?

Women's bags are typically shorter, wider at the hips, narrower at the shoulders, and have more insulation in the foot area. The temperature ratings account for women typically sleeping colder than men. Many women find unisex bags work fine, but women-specific bags optimize fit and warmth.

Do I need a sleeping bag liner?

Liners extend your bag's temperature range by 5-15 degrees, keep the interior clean, and add comfort. Silk liners add minimal weight, cotton liners add comfort, and synthetic liners balance performance and price. For frequently used bags, liners extend life by reducing cleaning frequency.
