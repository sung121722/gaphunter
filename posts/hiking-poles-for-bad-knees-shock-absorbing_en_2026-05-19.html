What Actually Matters When Choosing Hiking Poles for Bad Knees

  • Anti-shock spring mechanism — non-negotiable: A true anti-shock pole has an internal spring that compresses on each plant and releases as you push off. This absorbs 25–30% of the impact load that would otherwise travel up through your wrist, elbow, and into your knee. "Anti-shock" printed on packaging without a mechanical spring behind it means nothing. Squeeze the tip against a hard floor before you buy — you should feel the pole compress slightly. If it's rigid, it's not shock absorbing.
  • Pole height matters more than most people think: For knee protection on descents, you want your poles slightly longer than for flat terrain — not shorter. A common mistake is setting poles too short going downhill, which forces you into a hunched posture that loads your quads and knees unevenly. On descents, add 5–10cm to your standard flat-ground height. Adjustable poles between 110–130cm cover most hikers between 5'4" and 6'2".
  • Grip material and wrist strap quality: EVA foam handles win on long days because they conform to your hand and don't create hot spots the way hard cork or rubber can. The wrist strap should distribute load across your palm and forearm, not just hang loose. A well-fitted strap means you don't have to white-knuckle the grip on every plant — your wrist carries some of the work, reducing fatigue up the entire kinetic chain.
  • Folded pack size for real-world use: If the poles are annoying to carry when you're not using them, you'll use them less. Poles that fold or collapse to under 16 inches go in a side pocket. Poles that don't either lash to the outside of your pack (fine on clear trails, terrible in brush) or go unused. More usage = more knee protection. Simple math.
  • Pair technique with gear: The Knee Strengthening for Hikers and Walkers guide covers the science of using poles to offload 25% of your body weight from your knees — but only if your technique is correct. Plant the pole slightly forward on descents, use a diagonal rhythm, and engage your upper body. The poles do the work only if you let them. Consider picking up the guide alongside your poles for a complete approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do shock absorbing trekking poles actually help bad knees, or is it marketing?

They genuinely help — but only the ones with real mechanical anti-shock springs. Research and trail experience both point to a 25% reduction in knee load when poles are used correctly on descents. The keyword is "correctly": you need to plant the pole forward, keep your elbow slightly bent, and let the pole absorb the step rather than just tapping it along beside you. Budget poles with real spring mechanisms (like the VVHU at $24) deliver measurable relief. Poles labeled "anti-shock" without a spring? Skip those.

What pole height is best for protecting knees on steep downhills?

Set your poles 5–10cm longer than your flat-ground height when going downhill. Your flat-ground height is typically the point where your elbow is at 90 degrees with the pole tip on flat ground. On a steep descent, you want slightly more reach to maintain an upright posture and push your weight back, keeping load off the front of your knee. For most hikers between 5'6" and 6'0", that puts you somewhere between 120–128cm on the way down a significant grade.

Are aluminum or carbon fiber poles better for bad knees?

For knee protection specifically, the shock absorption mechanism matters more than the pole material. That said, aluminum poles like 7075 alloy handle repeated impact stress better than carbon fiber — carbon can crack under high lateral force, which is exactly the kind of force you generate when planting hard on uneven terrain. Carbon is lighter, but aluminum is more forgiving when you're relying on poles as actual support. For most hikers with bad knees, 7075 aluminum is the smarter call.

Can I use just one trekking pole instead of two for my bad knee?

You can, but two poles is significantly more effective. Using two poles distributes load symmetrically, which matters because compensating with one pole often creates an uneven gait that shifts extra stress onto your "good" knee — creating a second problem. Two poles also give you the anti-shock benefit on both sides of each step, cutting total impact load nearly in half compared to a single pole. If you're protecting a specific knee from a recent surgery or injury, position that side's pole slightly further forward than usual.
